Article 1

How to analyze your tennis serve at home

The easiest way to waste a serve review is to film from the wrong place and then expect perfect answers. Start with a stable phone or camera positioned behind the baseline or from a side angle that clearly shows loading, toss, contact, and follow-through. You do not need a professional setup. You need a clean view and enough repetition to show a pattern.

Before you upload, decide what you want answered. Are you missing first serves long? Losing rhythm under pressure? Struggling to generate pace? Context shapes how you read the result. SmartSwing is strongest when the player knows the goal, because the report can then be interpreted against something concrete instead of vague curiosity.

Once the analysis comes back, ignore the temptation to chase everything. A good report should narrow you toward the highest-cost issue first. If the serve chain is breaking because the load is shallow and the contact point is late, that matters more than polishing lower-impact details. Take one technical priority, one drill, and one tactical adjustment into the next session. That is where improvement starts to feel real.

Article 2

5 forehand mistakes AI can spot before they become habits

Forehand problems usually show up in clusters, not as one isolated flaw. The common ones are rushed spacing, a late unit turn, unstable contact height, weak loading into the outside leg, and a finish that does not match the intention of the ball. Left alone, those habits get reinforced because players often compensate just enough to stay functional in practice.

That is where AI feedback is useful. It helps identify which movement pattern is actually producing the miss instead of forcing the player to guess from feel alone. Article 3

USTA rating improvement guide: how to practice with more transfer

Most players trying to climb ratings do too much comfortable practice and not enough targeted correction. Rating jumps usually come from stronger decision quality, cleaner technique under pressure, and more reliable patterns on the balls that show up most in matches. That means practice has to connect to match transfer, not just repetition volume.

Use video and analysis to find the narrowest issue with the biggest competitive cost. Maybe you are losing short balls because your balance is poor moving forward. Maybe your second-serve pattern is predictable. Maybe you are neutralizing your own forehand with weak spacing. Once the issue is clear, the right drill block becomes easier to design and easier to stick with.

The point of technology is not to replace coaching judgment. It is to reduce wasted reps. If a player can leave every review knowing what changed, why it matters, and what to work on next, rating improvement becomes a more disciplined process instead of a hopeful one.
